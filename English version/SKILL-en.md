# Search Rule Transformation: Making Answers More Valuable

> Synthesized from a roundtable debate of 200 historical figures. Topic: "When someone asks a question, how can we transform the rules so the answer becomes more valuable?"

## Core Insight

Directly searching for "market gaps" or "industry opportunities" yields noise. A good search strategy requires **decomposing, reframing, and optimizing query rules**. What follows is the methodology distilled from the交锋 of 200 thinkers.

---

## I. Question Decomposition Rules

### 1.1 From "What Is" to "Under What Conditions"

**Source: Louis Pasteur**

Transform open-ended questions into falsifiable conditional questions.

| Bad Question | Good Question |
|-------------|---------------|
| What causes fermentation? | In sterile beet juice, does fermentation occur when yeast is introduced? Does it absolutely not occur without it? |
| What opportunities exist in the market? | Under the condition that Gen Z spending power grows 20%, which categories have penetration rates below 15%? |
| What jobs will AI replace? | When LLM costs drop to $0.001 per 1K tokens, which roles will see their ROI flip? |

**Rule: Break the vague "what is" into a causal chain of "Condition A + Condition B → Outcome C."**

### 1.2 Stress-Test Questioning

**Source: Niccolò Machiavelli**

Don't ask "How do I manage a team?" — this invites virtue signaling. Ask:

> "If your budget is cut 30% and a core team member publicly challenges your authority, what three steps will you take over the next three months? Include case studies from similar situations."

**Rule: Add constraints (time pressure, resource limits, conflicting situations) to the question to force answers out of abstraction.**

### 1.3 Negative Rules

**Source: Susan Sontag**

Rules shouldn't always prescribe "what must be included" — they should prescribe "what must be excluded":

- "Explain this concept without using any specialized terminology"
- "Don't quote any famous figures — derive it with your own logic"
- "No metaphors allowed — describe the mechanism directly"

**Rule: Use "prohibit" instead of "require" — strip away linguistic clichés and force the thinker back to the thing itself.**

## II. Information Filtering Rules

### 2.1 Prohibitive Rules Over Prescriptive Rules

**Source: F.A. Hayek**

Rather than mandating "You must use SWOT analysis," set "You must not deduce before defining core concepts."

| Prescriptive (narrows path) | Prohibitive (clears obstacles) |
|---------------------------|-------------------------------|
| Must use 5W1H analysis | Must not draw conclusions before specifying a time range |
| Must cite authoritative sources | Must not use unverified single sources |
| Must give specific numbers | Must not use vague quantifiers like "many" or "some" |

**Rule: Good search rules are prohibitive — they don't restrict where you look, but clear the obstacles in your thinking.**

### 2.2 Revealing Premises and Limitations

**Source: F.A. Hayek**

The most valuable rules require the respondent to reveal their premises:

- "State your core assumptions explicitly"
- "Identify the strongest evidence that could refute your position"
- "Under what conditions would your conclusion no longer hold?"

**Rule: Before searching, ask yourself: "What implicit assumptions lie behind my search terms?" Then actively search for counterexamples.**

### 2.3 Publicly Repeatable Verification Rules

**Source: Louis Pasteur**

Return the authority to judge answers from the mouths of experts to a carefully designed, publicly accessible verification rule:

- Not "experts say this market has potential," but "what is the search index growth rate for this category over the past 3 years?"
- Not "everyone is bullish on this direction," but "what is the trend in deal count and amount for this sector?"

**Rule: Replace opinion-based judgments with publicly verifiable data sources.**

## III. Search Strategy Transformation

### 3.1 From "Seeking Answers" to "Revealing Problems"

**Source: Susan Sontag**

| Seeking Answers (Low Value) | Revealing Problems (High Value) |
|---------------------------|-------------------------------|
| What's a good direction for a startup? | What problems do people endure daily that have never been solved? |
| Which industry has prospects? | Which industries have efficiency improvement rates far below technological advancement rates? |
| What skills are most valuable? | What skills have demand growing faster than supply, with declining barriers to entry? |

**Rule: Good search isn't about finding answers — it's about redefining the question.**

### 3.2 Strategic Context Matching

**Source: Henry Kissinger**

Rules must match the strategic context of the questioner:

- **Exploration phase**: Rules should remain open, using broad keywords to discover possibilities
- **Verification phase**: Rules should converge, using specific data sources and time ranges to lock down facts
- **Decision phase**: Rules should be ruthlessly demanding — require counterexamples, edge cases, failure cases

**Rule: Different search purposes require different query rules. There is no universal template.**

### 3.3 Meta-Rules: Rules That Generate New Rules

**Source: Henry Kissinger**

The best rules aren't fixed — they dynamically generate new rules:

- Unexpected signal in search results → automatically generate finer-grained sub-queries
- A particular data source proves information-dense → automatically dig deeper into related dimensions
- A search path yields nothing 3 consecutive times → automatically switch synonyms or change angle

**Rule: The search strategy itself should be adaptive, dynamically adjusting subsequent queries based on intermediate results.**

## IV. Anti-Bias Rules

### 4.1 Falsification First

**Source: Karl Popper**

Always reserve the highest seat for refutation. When searching:

1. First search: "Why would this direction fail?"
2. Then search: "What survivorship bias exists in success cases for this direction?"
3. Only then search: "What evidence supports this direction?"

**Rule: Find counterexamples first, then positive examples. If the counterexamples are strong enough, the positive examples cease to matter.**

### 4.2 Exposing Fundamental Tensions

**Source: Aaron Swartz (Synthesis)**

The value of rule transformation lies not in taking sides, but in **designing a rule that forcibly exposes and contains fundamental tensions**:

- "List the strongest arguments supporting this view, then list the strongest arguments opposing it"
- "If this conclusion is wrong, what's the most likely reason?"
- "At what time scale would this judgment reverse?"

**Rule: A good search strategy should simultaneously carry both sides, making the tension itself a source of insight.**

### 4.3 Self-Questioning Mechanism

**Source: Susan Sontag**

Any rule can become a new tyranny. Embed in the search strategy:

> "Please identify how the very set of search rules you are following might distort or obscure the question you are answering."

**Rule: Periodically audit your own search habits — which platforms do you always use? What keyword patterns do you default to? These habits themselves may be blind spots.**

## V. Practical Rule Checklist

### 5.1 Query Reformulation Template

| Original Question | Reformulation | Rationale |
|------------------|---------------|-----------|
| What is X? | Under what conditions does X hold? What are its counterexamples? | From definition to conditions |
| How large is the market for X? | Is the market for X's substitutes shrinking or growing? | From stock to trend |
| What opportunities exist in X? | What companies in X have closed in the last 3 years? Why? | From opportunities to failure cases |
| Is X's technical approach correct? | Under what assumptions would X's technical approach fail? | From verification to falsification |
| Who are X's users? | What alternatives are X's potential users using now? Why do they tolerate them? | From personas to pain points |

### 5.2 Search Depth Rules

1. **Surface search**: Keywords → search engine → first 3 pages of results
2. **Structured search**: Constrain data sources (industry reports, academic papers, patent databases) → constrain time range → constrain geography
3. **Reverse search**: Search for failure cases, causes of competitor death, policy risks
4. **Cross-validation**: Confirm the same conclusion across at least 3 independent data sources

### 5.3 Information Quality Filters

- **Verifiability**: Does the data have an original source? Can it be independently reproduced?
- **Timeliness**: When was the information produced? Does it still hold under current conditions?
- **Conflict of interest**: What vested interests does the information publisher have?
- **Sample bias**: Is this conclusion based on success cases or full-population data?

## VI. Metacognitive Rules

### 6.1 The Trap of Language

**Source: Ludwig Wittgenstein**

Language, when detached from the concrete situations where it does its work, goes "on holiday." Many searches fail because the search terms themselves are "on holiday" — they sound meaningful but point to no concrete operation.

| Language "On Holiday" | Concrete Language |
|----------------------|-------------------|
| Business model innovation | At which link, by what method, replacing what cost structure |
| User experience optimization | At which operational step is the drop-off rate highest, and what's the cause |
| Technical moat | What technical capability requires how much time and resources to acquire |

**Rule: Before searching, translate your question into a version with zero abstract words.**

### 6.2 The Cognitive Laboratory

**Source: John Rawls**

Rules can create an "original position" — a near-sterile thought laboratory:

- "If you knew nothing about this field, how would you search?"
- "If you could only use one data source to make a judgment, which would you choose? Why?"
- "If you had to make a decision within 24 hours, what would you search for?"

**Rule: Use hypothetical constraints to break search inertia.**

## Background

This skill is based on a roundtable debate of 200 historical figures, including but not limited to: Aristotle, Pasteur, Machiavelli, Hayek, Kissinger, Susan Sontag, Wittgenstein, Rawls, Popper, Newton, Kepler, Bohr, Aaron Swartz, Linus Torvalds, and others.

The debate used an elimination tournament format: 200 participants divided into 20 groups of 10, each group going through 4 rounds of structured debate (Position → Critique → Deepening → Closing). Winners advanced, and a final public vote determined the synthesizer.

**Final Winner: Aaron Swartz** (100 votes to Linus Torvalds' 100 votes, tie; won on tiebreak)
